In today's media landscape, producers and designers need to be thinking about designing a user experience, not a static product. The goal is to anticipate how users/readers/viewers might want to consume information and design an interface that will lead to a seamless experience. Designers need to understand what it means to be an information architect. They need to have a firm grasp of reader behavior, navigation and interface design. As sophisticated multimedia journalists, they need to know when multimedia adds value and contributes to storytelling and when it simply slows load time and clutters a project.

About J4502

In this class, you will learn to ask and answer these questions:

  • How does this story or strategic message want to be told or conveyed? Does it have a beginning, middle and end, or is it non-linear? What media are most appropriate to use? And what platform? We will learn to stage and frame our packages.
  • How might people want to consume the information I'm offering? It's important to realize that we can't tell someone how to consume our story or product. We must learn to anticipate the many perfectly valid reading habits users might bring to the table, and be ready to accommodate a variety of reader behaviors.
  • How can I apply the basics of good information design (typography, color, grid, images, etc.) and learn to marry them with motion and sound?
  • How can I innovate in my storytelling? We'll look at ways to move beyond the usual — beyond the arranging of elements — and use interactivity to help redefine the idea of story or product.
  • How can I incorporate web standards and best practices into my web design work? It doesn't matter how brilliant your multimedia projects/sites are if they take 10 minutes to load or aren't accessible.

Software

This is not a class in software. It's a class in concepts and critical thinking and in how to plan multimedia packages. While we will go over the highlights of some of these skills and programs in class, you'll learn most of what you need to know on your own. You will learn to execute your ideas using tools that are common in today's workplace. However, know that it's crucial that you be able to quickly adapt your process as the tools and conventions change. You will learn to execute your projects in XHMTL and CSS, using Dreamweaver or the HTML editor of your choice. Additionally, you'll learn how to add more interactivity to your sites using HTML5, jQuery and CSS3. You will also do mockups of your sites in a layout program (ex: InDesign, Illustrator, Photoshop).
